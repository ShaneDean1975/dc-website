### Main Content Outline:

**Introduction:**
Property owners - whether businesses, landlords, or private homeowners - have a legal duty to maintain reasonably safe premises for visitors. When dangerous conditions cause injuries, victims have the right to seek compensation through premises liability claims. These cases require proving the property owner knew or should have known about the hazard and failed to fix it or warn visitors.

At Dean & Camper, P.A., we've successfully represented numerous clients injured on unsafe properties throughout Pensacola, Destin, Fort Walton Beach, Mobile, and the Gulf Coast. We know how to prove property owner negligence and overcome their defenses.

**Property Owner Duties in Florida & Alabama:**

**Florida Law:**
- Duty owed depends on visitor status (invitee, licensee, trespasser)
- Invitees: Business customers owed highest duty of care
- Must warn of or fix known dangerous conditions
- Must inspect for hazards regularly
- Comparative negligence may reduce damages

**Alabama Law:**
- Similar duty based on visitor status
- Property owners must exercise reasonable care
- Must warn of hidden dangers
- Contributory negligence can bar recovery (harsh rule)
- Exceptions for wanton or willful conduct

**Proving a Premises Liability Claim:**
Successful claims require proving:
1. **Dangerous Condition Existed** - Hazard on the property
2. **Owner Had Notice** - Actual or constructive knowledge of hazard
3. **Failure to Fix or Warn** - Owner didn't remedy or warn visitors
4. **Causation** - Condition caused your injury
5. **Damages** - You suffered actual harm

**Actual vs. Constructive Notice:**
- **Actual Notice:** Owner knew about the hazard
- **Constructive Notice:** Hazard existed long enough that owner should have known
- Proving constructive notice often requires evidence of how long condition existed

**Negligent Security Claims:**

**Florida Dog Bite Law - Strict Liability:**

Florida Statute 767.04 imposes strict liability:
- **Dog owner is liable** regardless of dog's prior behavior
- **No "one free bite" rule** - even first-time bites create liability
- **No need to prove negligence** - strict liability statute
- **Exceptions:** Trespassing, comparative negligence if victim provoked dog

This makes Florida one of the most victim-friendly states for dog bite cases.

**Alabama Dog Bite Law - Scienter (One-Bite Rule):**

Alabama follows common law "one-bite rule":
- **Must prove owner knew or should have known dog was dangerous**
- Prior bites, aggressive behavior, breed reputation can establish knowledge
- Alternative: **Negligence** - owner failed to properly restrain dog
- Alternative: **Premises liability** - dangerous condition on property

Alabama law is less favorable to victims but claims are still possible.

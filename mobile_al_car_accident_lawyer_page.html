Common Accidents in Mobile

Mobile's unique geography and infrastructure contribute to a high volume of serious accidents. Major highways, waterway crossings, and industrial zones create hazardous conditions that lead to injuries every day. The most common accident locations and scenarios in the Mobile area include:

  • I-10 and I-65 Collisions: These two major interstates intersect near Mobile, carrying heavy volumes of passenger vehicles and commercial trucks. High-speed rear-end crashes, lane-change accidents, and multi-vehicle pileups are frequent, especially during rush hour and inclement weather
  • The Bayway (I-10 Bayway): The elevated stretch of I-10 crossing Mobile Bay is one of the most dangerous corridors in Alabama. Narrow lanes, limited shoulders, and fog create conditions that lead to serious and sometimes fatal crashes
  • The Causeway (US-98): Connecting Mobile to the Eastern Shore communities of Spanish Fort, Daphne, and Fairhope, the Causeway sees heavy daily commuter traffic and frequent head-on and rear-end collisions
  • Airport Boulevard and Schillinger Road: These high-traffic corridors in western Mobile are lined with commercial businesses and see a disproportionate number of intersection accidents, including T-bone crashes and pedestrian injuries
  • Port and Industrial Area Truck Accidents: The Port of Mobile is one of the busiest on the Gulf Coast. Heavy truck traffic traveling to and from the port, along with industrial vehicles on surrounding roads, creates serious risks for passenger vehicle drivers
  • Government Street and Downtown Mobile: Pedestrian accidents, bicycle collisions, and low-speed vehicle crashes are common in Mobile's historic downtown area, particularly near restaurants, entertainment venues, and the convention center

Alabama vs. Florida Personal Injury Law -- Key Differences

Dean & Camper P.A. represents injury victims in both Alabama and Florida. Understanding the differences between these two states' laws is critical for anyone injured in Mobile:

Statute of Limitations: Alabama gives you only 2 years from the date of your accident to file a personal injury lawsuit. Florida provides a longer window of 4 years for most personal injury claims. Missing the deadline in either state means losing your right to pursue compensation entirely.

Contributory vs. Comparative Negligence: This is the most important distinction. Alabama is one of only a handful of states that follows a pure contributory negligence rule. If you are found to be even 1% at fault for the accident, you may be completely barred from recovering any compensation. Florida uses a comparative negligence system, where your compensation is reduced by your percentage of fault but you can still recover damages. Because Alabama's rule is so harsh, having an experienced attorney who can defend against fault allegations is essential to protecting your claim.

Insurance Requirements: Alabama is an at-fault state, meaning the driver who caused the accident is responsible for paying damages. Florida is a no-fault state that requires drivers to carry Personal Injury Protection (PIP) insurance. These differences affect how claims are filed and what compensation is available to you.

Compensation for Mobile Accident Victims

If you have been injured in an accident in Mobile, Alabama, you may be entitled to recover compensation for:

  • Medical expenses: Emergency care, surgery, hospitalization, rehabilitation, prescription medications, and future medical treatment
  • Lost wages: Income lost during your recovery period
  • Lost earning capacity: Diminished ability to earn a living due to permanent injuries
  • Pain and suffering: Physical pain, emotional distress, and mental anguish caused by the accident
  • Property damage: Vehicle repair or replacement and personal property losses
  • Loss of enjoyment of life: Inability to participate in activities and hobbies you enjoyed before the injury
  • Wrongful death damages: Funeral and burial expenses, loss of financial support, and loss of companionship for surviving family members

Common Accidents in Crestview

Crestview's location at the crossroads of major highways and its proximity to military installations create unique accident risks. Our attorneys handle cases involving:

  • Interstate 10 (I-10) -- The main east-west interstate through Crestview carries heavy commercial truck traffic and sees high-speed collisions, jackknife accidents, and multi-vehicle pileups, particularly during storms and fog.
  • Highway 85 (South Ferdon Boulevard) -- The primary north-south corridor connecting Crestview to Niceville and the coast is one of the most heavily traveled roads in the county, with frequent rear-end and intersection collisions.
  • Highway 90 (James Lee Boulevard) -- This east-west commercial corridor through downtown Crestview sees constant stop-and-go traffic and a high rate of distracted driving accidents near shopping areas.
  • Eglin AFB and Duke Field Traffic -- Military personnel commuting to and from base during shift changes create heavy traffic surges on Highway 85 and surrounding roads, leading to congestion-related crashes.
  • Logging Truck Accidents -- Northern Okaloosa County has significant timber industry activity, and logging trucks on rural highways present serious hazards including wide-load accidents and debris-related crashes.
  • Rural Road Accidents -- The unincorporated areas around Crestview include narrow two-lane roads with limited lighting, sharp curves, and wildlife crossings that contribute to nighttime and single-vehicle accidents.

Whether your accident happened on a busy interstate or a quiet county road, our team will investigate the circumstances, identify all responsible parties, and pursue every dollar of compensation you deserve.

Florida and Alabama Personal Injury Laws

Florida: Florida is a no-fault insurance state. Your own Personal Injury Protection (PIP) coverage pays for initial medical expenses regardless of fault. However, if your injuries are serious -- involving significant scarring, permanent injury, or disability -- you can file a claim directly against the at-fault driver. Florida applies a modified comparative negligence rule: your compensation is reduced by your percentage of fault, and you cannot recover if you are more than 50% responsible. The statute of limitations for personal injury in Florida is 2 years from the date of the accident.

Alabama: Alabama follows a strict contributory negligence rule, one of the most restrictive in the country. If you are found even 1% at fault, you may be completely barred from recovering compensation. Alabama's statute of limitations is also 2 years. Because of this harsh standard, having skilled legal representation is critical for any injury claim filed in Alabama.

Our firm is licensed in both states and understands how to navigate these different legal frameworks to protect your claim and maximize your recovery.
